Section 1: What Is IPE?

Where politics meets the economy – and why we can’t study them apart

The Opening Puzzle

You are shopping for a new phone. The price, the brand, even whether that phone exists at all – every step involves political decisions made by governments you never voted for. How?

  • Tariffs affect the price you pay
  • Trade agreements determine which companies can sell here
  • Labor laws abroad shape where the phone gets assembled
  • Currency exchange rates set the cost of imported components

Your consumer experience is shaped by international politics at every level.

What Is International Political Economy?

International Political Economy (IPE)

Definition The study of the interplay of economics and politics in the world arena

  • Economics = the system of producing, distributing, and using wealth
  • Politics = the institutions and rules governing social and economic interactions
  • These two spheres are in constant mutual interaction

Source: Frieden & Lake, International Political Economy (2000), p. 1

Why Did IPE Emerge?

Before 1970, economics and political science were studied almost entirely separately.

  • Economists built models of pure markets
  • Political scientists built theories of pure politics
  • Then the 1970s happened: oil shocks, the end of the gold-dollar standard, global recession
  • Suddenly, ignoring the connection between economics and politics became impossible

The Bretton Woods Order

  1. 1944 — Bretton Woods Agreement creates the IMF, World Bank, and a gold-backed dollar system
  2. 1947 — GATT established to liberalize trade among Western nations
  3. 1950s–60s — The “Pax Americana”: rapid growth under U.S. economic and military leadership
  4. 1971 — Nixon ends the gold-dollar standard; the monetary system begins to unravel
  5. 1973–75 — OPEC oil shock + global recession; economics and politics collide

Examples?

Can you think of any recent events that illustrate the connection between economics and politics?

Four Ways to Think About IPE

Where do explanations come from?

  • International level: global power structures, the system itself constrains state behavior
  • Domestic level: interest groups, elections, and national institutions drive policy

Who drives policy?

  • State-centered: leaders and institutions make autonomous decisions
  • Society-centered: organized groups, firms, and voters push governments to act

These two dimensions create four distinct perspectives. Most real-world explanations draw on more than one.

Section 2: Trade Politics

Who wins, who loses, and why everyone fights about it

Trade: The Basic Insight

“The most famous insight from all of international economics is the proof that trade provides mutual gains: that is, when countries exchange goods and services they are all generally better off.”

— Michael J. Hiscox, “The Domestic Sources of Foreign Economic Policies,” p. 52

So if trade makes everyone better off in aggregate, why is it one of the most politically explosive issues in every country?

The Answer: Trade Creates Winners and Losers

Comparative Advantage

Definition Each country benefits from specializing in goods it produces at lower opportunity cost relative to other countries, then trading for everything else.

  • Aggregate gains are real: the total pie grows
  • But specific groups within each country lose out
  • The political fight is over distribution, not efficiency

Source: Hiscox, p. 52

Who Wins? Two Competing Models

Model 1: Stolper-Samuelson (Factor Model)

  • Trade benefits owners of abundant factors (land, labor, or capital)
  • Trade hurts owners of scarce factors
  • Politics divides along class lines: labor vs. capital, landowners vs. workers

Who Wins? Two Competing Models

Model 1: Stolper-Samuelson (Factor Model)

  • Trade benefits owners of abundant factors (land, labor, or capital)
  • Trade hurts owners of scarce factors
  • Politics divides along class lines: labor vs. capital, landowners vs. workers

Model 2: Ricardo-Viner (Specific Factors Model)

  • What matters is not what type of factor you own, but which industry you work in
  • Export industries benefit; import-competing industries lose
  • Politics divides along industry lines: steel workers vs. tech workers

Stolper-Samuelson in Plain English

“Trade benefits those who own the factors of production with which the economy is relatively well endowed and trade hurts owners of scarce factors.”

— Michael J. Hiscox, “The Domestic Sources of Foreign Economic Policies,” p. 53

  • The U.S. is capital-abundant and labor-scarce (relative to, say, China)
  • So free trade tends to benefit American capital owners and hurt American workers
  • In China, the reverse holds: workers gain, capital owners face competition

Side by Side: Two Models of Trade Politics

Stolper-Samuelson

  • Cleavage: class-based (labor vs. capital)
  • Coalitions: all workers vs. all capital owners
  • Best explains: 19th-century British politics (Corn Laws repeal)
  • Prediction: in the U.S., workers oppose trade, capital owners support it

Ricardo-Viner

  • Cleavage: industry-based (export vs. import-competing)
  • Coalitions: workers AND owners in the same industry align together
  • Best explains: modern trade politics (steel workers + steel firms vs. tech)
  • Prediction: trade politics fractures by sector, not class

Discussion

Think about your own family or community. Would free trade with China benefit or hurt the people you know? Would they divide along class lines (Stolper-Samuelson) or industry lines (Ricardo-Viner)?

Historical Case: The Smoot-Hawley Tariff, 1930

In 1930, the U.S. Congress passed the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act, altering duties on over 20,000 items. The Senate alone made 1,253 amendments to the original House bill (Hiscox, p. 69).

  • Protectionist log-rolling: each legislator traded votes to protect their district’s industry
  • Retaliation was swift: other countries raised their own tariffs
  • Result: world trade collapsed, deepening the Great Depression
  • Legacy: Congress delegated trade authority to the President (the RTAA of 1934) to prevent it from happening again

Institutions Shape Who Wins

“There is really no such thing as the ‘national interest’ when it comes to foreign economic policy – or, rather there is no one national interest, there are many.”

— Michael J. Hiscox, “The Domestic Sources of Foreign Economic Policies,” p. 72

  • Who votes matters: expanding suffrage changed trade politics in Britain and the U.S.
  • Electoral systems matter: proportional representation reduces protectionist lobbying
  • Who sets the agenda matters: delegating trade authority to the executive reduced log-rolling

Section 3: Globalization and Its Discontents

Why free trade keeps winning the economic argument but losing the political one

The Everyday Impact

How many of you checked the price of groceries this week? Gas? Rent?

  • When the price of imported goods rises (due to tariffs), consumers pay more
  • When a factory closes because production moved overseas, workers lose jobs
  • Both effects are real, but they hit different people
  • Trade policy is a fight between concentrated losers (specific industries) and diffuse winners (all consumers paying lower prices)

Contemporary Case: The U.S.-China Trade War

Beginning in 2018, the Trump administration imposed escalating rounds of tariffs that by 2019 covered roughly $360 billion in Chinese imports. China retaliated with tariffs on U.S. agricultural exports.

  • Winners: some U.S. steel and aluminum producers saw short-term gains
  • Losers: U.S. farmers lost access to China’s soybean market; consumers faced higher prices on electronics, furniture, clothing
  • Bipartisan continuity: the Biden administration kept most tariffs in place
  • 2025 escalation: tariffs have expanded under the second Trump administration

Rodrik’s Warning: Globalization Fuels Populism

Dani Rodrik (2020) identifies four channels through which globalization drives populist politics:

  • (A) Direct economic dislocation: workers who lose jobs demand anti-elite policies
  • (B) Identity activation: economic insecurity sharpens “us vs. them” divisions
  • (C) Party repositioning: politicians adopt more populist platforms in response to economic shocks
  • (D) Strategic distraction: political candidates inflame cultural and identity tensions to shift voters’ attention from economic issues

The Backlash Goes Global

Bonifai, Nooruddin, and Rudra (2021) add a twist: the globalization backlash is spreading to the developing world too.

  • In Mexico, support for free trade among low-skilled workers dropped by 20 percentage points (2002–2018)
  • In Brazil, support fell from 84% to 68%
  • The promise was that trade would bring widespread upward mobility
  • The reality: gains concentrated among the already skilled

The anger in developing countries mirrors the anger in rich ones, for the same structural reason: trade benefits are unevenly distributed.

The Student-Accessible Case: Your Job Market

After you graduate, will globalization help or hurt your job prospects?

  • If you enter tech, finance, or consulting: globalization expands your market
  • If you enter manufacturing, clerical work, or customer service: automation and offshoring may threaten your position
  • Your college degree is itself a factor endowment that shapes your trade preferences
  • The Stolper-Samuelson model predicts that high-skilled workers in the U.S. benefit from trade openness

Discussion

If trade makes the aggregate economy better off but harms specific groups, does the government have an obligation to compensate the losers? Who should pay for that?

Synthesis

Pulling the threads together

Summary

Today’s key insights:

  • IPE studies how economics and politics shape each other
  • Trade produces aggregate gains but creates specific winners and losers, and that distributional conflict drives politics
  • The Stolper-Samuelson model predicts class-based trade coalitions; the Ricardo-Viner model predicts industry-based coalitions
  • Institutions (electoral systems, legislative rules, executive authority) determine which groups get their way
  • Globalization’s uneven gains are fueling populist backlash in both rich and developing countries
