Course Review: Second Half

Civil Wars, Terrorism, and the Search for Peace

  • Walter: the ICT revolution reshaped civil wars. Transnational recruitment, extreme ideologies as commitment devices, conflicts that resist borders and settlement

  • Toft: negotiated settlements fail without credible enforcement. Rebel victories produce surprisingly durable peace. Effective settlements need carrots and sticks

  • Crenshaw: terrorism is rational organizational strategy, born from elite disaffection in contexts of blocked participation. Provocation logic: terrorists want the state to overreact

  • Common thread: commitment problems keep showing up

International Political Economy

  • IPE studies how economics and politics shape each other

  • Trade produces aggregate gains but creates winners and losers

  • Stolper-Samuelson: trade politics divides along class lines (abundant vs. scarce factors)

  • Ricardo-Viner: trade politics divides along industry lines (export vs. import-competing sectors)

  • Institutions (electoral systems, executive authority) determine which groups get their way

  • Globalization’s uneven gains fuel populist backlash in both rich and developing countries (Rodrik)

International Organizations and International Law

  • Keohane’s functional theory: self-interested states create institutions to reduce transaction costs, lower uncertainty, and raise the cost of cheating

  • IOs range from technical bodies (UPU) to the UN system, each facing tradeoffs between effectiveness and legitimacy

  • P5 veto reflects 1945 power realities; growing demands for reform

  • International law works through treaties, custom, and judicial decisions, enforced by reputation and reciprocity, not coercion

  • Compliance is high in routine cases; breaks down when great-power interests are at stake (South China Sea, ICC)

Transnational Authoritarianism

  • Post-Cold War optimism that liberal democracy had “won” proved premature: a third wave of autocratization is underway

  • Autocracy Inc. (Applebaum): autocracies cooperate across borders through financial, security, and propaganda networks, linked by deals, not ideology

  • Asymmetric openness (Cooley & Nexon): autocracies exploit the liberal order’s openness while insulating their own politics

  • Sharp power (Walker & Ludwig): media partnerships, university funding, tech standards, corporate leverage

  • But authoritarian resilience has limits: Assad fell, Maduro was removed (regime persists), Orban lost at the ballot box

Existential Risk

  • Nuclear taboo (Tannenwald): a normative prohibition, not just deterrence, explains 80 years of non-use. Schelling counters: it is a self-reinforcing focal point, not a moral achievement

  • Deterrence alone cannot explain non-use against non-nuclear states

  • Existential risk (Ord, MacAskill): we live at the beginning of human history, not the end.

  • AI, engineered pandemics, and climate change pose threats that existing institutions were not built to handle

  • Governance gap: nuclear regime is mature; climate regime is weak; bioweapons and AI regimes are hollow or nonexistent

Climate and Emerging Risks

  • Climate change is the hardest collective action problem: caused by everyone, harms are delayed, worst impacts fall on those who contributed least

  • Patrick: the multilateral system treats ecology as secondary.

  • Satia: climate discourse is shaped by power. Asking developing countries to “sacrifice” assumes equal starting points

  • AI makes the security dilemma worse than nuclear weapons: harder to verify, lower barriers to entry, faster development

What This Course Was About

How do independent actors in a system with no central authority manage to cooperate, compete, and survive?

  • Sometimes through institutions, law, and norms
  • Sometimes through power, coercion, and hierarchy
  • Sometimes they fail: wars, exploitation, democratic collapse, existential risk
  • The frameworks you learned help you ask better questions about all of these
